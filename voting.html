Right to Vote in Haiti Constitution

Article 52 of the 1987 Constitution guarantees the right to vote:

"All citizens who are eighteen (18) years of age and who enjoy civil and political rights have the right to vote. Exercise of the right to vote is obligatory."

This means:

  • Voting is a RIGHT – no one can prevent you from voting
  • Voting is also a DUTY – every citizen should participate
  • You must be 18 or older
  • You must enjoy your civil rights (not in prison, not banned by court)

Who Can Vote in Haiti?

Conditions to Vote

To vote in Haiti, you must:

  • Be 18 or older
  • Be a Haitian citizen (born Haitian or naturalized)
  • Be registered on the electoral list (registered to vote)
  • Have National ID Card (CIN) or other legal document
  • Not be in prison for serious crime (loss of civil rights)

Who Cannot Vote?

  • Children under 18
  • Foreigners who are not Haitian citizens
  • People who lost civil rights by court decision
  • People convicted of certain serious crimes

How to Register to Vote?

Documents You Need

To register, you need:

  • Birth Certificate: To prove your age and citizenship
  • Baptism Certificate: Accepted if you don't have birth certificate
  • Tax ID Card (NIF): Tax identification
  • Driver's License: If you have one
  • Passport: If you have one

NOTE: The document system varies. The Provisional Electoral Council (CEP) declares which documents are accepted.

Where to Register?

Voter registration happens at:

  • Local CEP offices (Provisional Electoral Council)
  • Special registration centers during enrollment periods
  • Sometimes, schools or community centers converted to registration centers

You must register in the community where you live. You cannot vote if you're not registered!

What Types of Elections Are in Haiti?

Presidential Election

  • Frequency: Every 5 years
  • Term: President serves for 5 years
  • Limit: Cannot serve two consecutive terms
  • System: Two rounds if no candidate gets 50%+1 in first round

Legislative Elections

Senate (Upper Chamber):

  • 30 Senators (3 per department)
  • 6-year term
  • 1/3 renewed every 2 years

Chamber of Deputies (Lower Chamber):

  • 119 Deputies
  • 4-year term
  • All renewed at the same time

Local Elections

  • Municipal Council: Mayor, assistant, and local council members
  • KASEC/ASEC: Community section authorities
  • Departmental Council: Department representatives

Voting Process: Election Day

Before You Go Vote

  • Verify you're registered on the electoral list
  • Know where your polling center is (where you must vote)
  • Bring your ID document (CIN, passport, or other legal ID)
  • Inform yourself about candidates and their platforms

At the Polling Center

  1. Arrive early: Centers open 6am and close 4pm (varies by election)
  2. Find your polling station: Each center has multiple stations. Look for your name on the list.
  3. Present your ID: Show observers your documents
  4. Receive your ballot: You'll get a ballot for each position
  5. Go to the booth: Choose your candidate in private
  6. Mark your ballot: Check, mark X, or fold ballot per instructions
  7. Put ballot in box: Fold your ballot and put it in the ballot box
  8. Sign the register: Put your signature or fingerprint to confirm you voted

After Voting

  • They will mark your finger with ink (to show you already voted)
  • You can stay to watch the vote count if you want (public observation)
  • Follow results on radio, TV, or social media

Electoral System in Haiti

Absolute Majority (President)

For presidential elections:

  • First Round: Candidate who gets 50%+1 wins directly
  • Second Round: If no one gets 50%, top two candidates go to second round
  • Win: In second round, candidate with most votes wins

Plurality (Legislative, Local)

For legislative and local elections:

  • Candidate with most votes in a constituency wins
  • Doesn't need 50% – just more votes than others
  • Single round

Provisional Electoral Council (CEP)

CEP is the organization that manages elections in Haiti. According to the 1987 Constitution:

CEP Members

  • 9 total members
  • Chosen by different sectors of society (religious, professional, education, etc.)
  • 3-year term
  • Independent (not controlled by government)

CEP Responsibilities

  • Organize all elections
  • Register voters
  • Register candidates
  • Establish polling stations
  • Count votes
  • Publish results
  • Resolve electoral disputes

Electoral Challenges in Haiti

Insecurity

Armed gangs control parts of the country, making it difficult to:

  • People vote safely
  • Open polling centers in dangerous areas
  • Observers monitor elections

Lack of Infrastructure

  • Many areas lack easy access for registration
  • Polling centers too far in rural areas
  • Lack of electoral materials (ballots, boxes, ink)

Political Crisis

  • Since 2020, no functioning Parliament
  • Elections postponed multiple times
  • Government without electoral mandate

Lack of Funding

Organizing elections is expensive. In October 2025, CEP declared it impossible to hold elections before February 2026 due to lack of money.

Lack of Trust

  • People have no trust in CEP
  • Repeated accusations of electoral fraud
  • Low participation (often less than 20% vote)

Electoral Fraud: What Is It?

Electoral fraud is illegal manipulation of elections. Examples:

Before Election

  • Preventing registration: Stop people from registering
  • Vote buying: Pay or threaten people to vote for a candidate
  • Intimidation: Scare voters

Election Day

  • Voting multiple times: Same person votes at multiple stations
  • Voting in place of others: Use papers of dead or absent people
  • Controlling polling stations: Gangs or political groups occupy stations
  • Ballot stuffing: Put fraudulent ballots in box before or after voting

After Election

  • Manipulating count: Change results during counting
  • Destroying ballots: Throw away votes that don't match desired results
  • Making false results: Publish numbers that don't reflect real votes

If you see fraud: Report it to CEP, independent observers, or civil rights organizations.

Next Elections in Haiti

Current Status (December 2025):

  • Elections were planned for November 15, 2025 and January 2026
  • CEP declared it impossible to organize them before February 2026
  • Reasons: gang insecurity, lack of funding, lack of electoral infrastructure
  • Over 220 political parties registered
  • CEP recruiting staff and preparing electoral lists

Exact dates will be announced by CEP when conditions permit.

How to Prepare to Vote

1. Register Now

  • Don't wait until last minute
  • Gather your documents now
  • Find nearest CEP office

2. Inform Yourself

  • Read candidate platforms
  • Watch debates
  • Research candidates (history, promises, past actions)
  • Follow political news

3. Discuss with Others

  • Talk with family, friends, neighbors
  • Participate in community forums
  • Listen to different viewpoints

4. Verify Your Registration

  • A few weeks before election, confirm your name on list
  • Know exactly where your polling center is

5. Plan for Election Day

  • Prepare your documents the night before
  • Plan your transportation
  • Free time to go vote

Why Don't Many People Vote?

Reasons They Give

  • "My vote doesn't count" – no trust in the system
  • "All politicians are the same" – don't see difference
  • "I'm too busy" – no time
  • "I'm not registered" – too complicated to register
  • "Area not secure" – fear violence
  • "I don't understand politics" – no civic education

The Reality

If you don't vote:

  • Others will make decisions for you
  • Politicians won't care about your interests
  • You abandon your power
  • Difficult to complain afterward

Change starts when everyone participates. If the majority doesn't vote, a minority makes all the decisions.

Voting and Electoral Law

Haiti's electoral law defines:

  • How to organize elections
  • Rights and responsibilities of candidates
  • Campaign financing rules
  • Procedures for disputes and complaints
  • Sanctions for violations

Electoral law changes often. Always follow official CEP publications for current information.

Conclusion

Voting is one of the most powerful tools citizens have to change the country. But it's only effective if we all participate.

Every vote counts. Every voice matters. In a country where the majority doesn't vote, a small minority decides for everyone.

Your vote is your voice. Use it!
