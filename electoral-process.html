Pwosesis Elektoral

Dènye mizajou: 16 desanm 2025

  • Acceptance: All parties accept legal results

    • Provisional Electoral Council (CEP)

    Role and Responsibilities

    CEP is the institution that manages all elections in Haiti. According to the 1987 Constitution (Articles 191-201), CEP is responsible for:

    • Organizing all national and local elections
    • Registering citizens on electoral lists
    • Accepting or rejecting political party candidates
    • Establishing polling centers and polling stations
    • Monitoring the voting process
    • Counting votes and publishing results
    • Resolving electoral disputes

    CEP Composition

    CEP has 9 members:

    • Chosen by different sectors of society (religious, professional, university, agriculture, journalists, human rights, unions, and Executive)
    • 3-year term (renewable)
    • Must be independent – not holding other public or private function
    • Work collectively – decisions by majority

    Challenges and Criticism

    CEP faces major problems:

    • Lack of Independence: Members often chosen by political considerations
    • Limited Resources: Lack of money to function properly
    • Lack of Trust: People don't trust CEP due to fraud suspicions
    • Insecurity: Gangs prevent organizing elections in some areas
    • Limited Infrastructure: No modern equipment for electoral management

    Types of Elections in Haiti

    Presidential Election

    Frequency: Every 5 years

    Term: President serves for 5 years

    System: Two rounds

    • First Round: All candidates compete. If one candidate gets 50% + 1 votes, they win directly
    • Second Round: If no one gets absolute majority, top two candidates go to second round. Candidate with most votes wins

    Candidate conditions:

    • At least 35 years old
    • Native-born Haitian (born Haitian, not naturalized)
    • Never taken other citizenship
    • Property owner in Haiti
    • Resident in Haiti for at least 5 consecutive years before election

    Legislative Elections

    Senate (Upper Chamber):

    • 30 total senators (3 per department)
    • 6-year term
    • 1/3 renewed every 2 years
    • System: One round – candidate with most votes wins
    • Conditions: 25 years minimum, Haitian, literate

    Chamber of Deputies (Lower Chamber):

    • 119 total deputies
    • 4-year term
    • All renewed at same time
    • System: One round – candidate with most votes wins
    • Conditions: 25 years minimum, Haitian, literate

    Local Elections

    Municipal Council (Mayor and Council):

    • 4-year term
    • Mayor and assistant lead commune
    • Municipal council votes on local projects

    ASEC/KASEC (Community Section Authorities):

    • Rural community representatives
    • Manage some local services

    Departmental Council:

    • Represent departments
    • Work on regional development

    Voter Registration Process

    Who Can Register?

    To register to vote, you must:

    • Be at least 18 years old
    • Be a Haitian citizen
    • Not in prison for serious crime (loss of civil rights)
    • Not banned by court

    Necessary Documents

    To register, you need one of these documents:

    • Birth Certificate: Prove age and citizenship
    • Baptism Certificate: Accepted if no birth certificate
    • National ID Card (CIN): Official identity card
    • Tax ID Card (NIF): National tax number
    • Driver's License: If you have one
    • Passport: If you have one

    Where and How to Register?

    • Local CEP offices
    • Special registration centers during registration period
    • You must register in community where you live
    • After registration, you receive voter card or your name added to electoral list

    Registration Problems

    • Many Haitians don't have identity documents
    • Registration offices too far in rural areas
    • Complicated and bureaucratic process
    • Gangs control some areas where people can't go register
    • Electoral lists not updated – dead or gone people still on lists

    Electoral Campaign Process

    Campaign Period

    Electoral campaign starts several weeks before election day. CEP establishes specific rules for each election:

    • Start and end dates of campaign
    • Rules on campaign finance (spending limits, money sources)
    • Rules on propaganda (where to post signs, media use)
    • Prohibitions (vote buying, violence, disinformation)

    Campaign Activities

    During campaign, political parties and candidates:

    • Organize meetings and rallies
    • Distribute campaign materials (posters, flyers, t-shirts)
    • Use media (radio, TV, social networks)
    • Hold public debates
    • Visit communities door-to-door

    Campaign Problems in Haiti

    • Vote Buying: Distributing money, food, roofing for votes
    • Violence: Gangs attack opposition rallies
    • Disinformation: False propaganda on social media
    • Resource Inequality: Rich parties have more means to campaign
    • Lack of Access: Gang-controlled areas, candidates can't campaign

    Election Day: Voting Process

    Pre-Voting Preparation

    CEP prepares:

    • Establish polling centers (schools, churches, community centers)
    • Recruit and train observers and electoral staff
    • Distribute materials (ballots, ballot boxes, ink)
    • Publish list of polling centers and electoral lists

    Voting Process (Election Day)

    Opening Polling Centers:

    • Centers open at 6am (may vary)
    • Observers and party representatives verify materials
    • Ballot boxes opened in front of everyone to show they're empty

    During Voting:

    1. Voter arrives with identity document
    2. Observer verifies voter name on electoral list
    3. Voter receives ballot
    4. Voter goes to booth to mark ballot privately
    5. Voter folds ballot and puts it in ballot box
    6. Voter signs register or puts fingerprint
    7. Voter's finger marked with ink to prevent voting twice

    Closing Polling Centers:

    • Centers close at 4pm (may vary)
    • People already in line can vote even if time passed
    • Boxes sealed in front of observers

    Voting Day Security

    Security measures to protect voting integrity:

    • Police or security forces present (if possible)
    • National and international observers monitor
    • Political party representatives at each center
    • Media monitors process

    Vote Counting and Publishing Results

    Counting at Polling Centers

    After centers close:

    • Observers open boxes in front of everyone
    • Count ballots one by one with transparency
    • Write results on minutes sheet
    • All observers and representatives sign minutes
    • Post results at polling center for public to see

    Transmitting Results

    • Minutes sent to central CEP
    • CEP collects results from all polling centers
    • Verify consistency between documents
    • Identify irregularities or problems

    Publishing Results

    • CEP publishes provisional results after counting
    • Complaint period (candidates can protest)
    • CEP resolves complaints and disputes
    • Publish official final results

    Counting and Results Problems

    • Slow or non-transparent counting
    • Minutes disappear or manipulated
    • Differences between local count and CEP count
    • Delays in publishing results create suspicion
    • Lack of total transparency on counting

    Electoral Observation

    Who Observes?

    Several actors monitor elections:

    • National Observers: Haitian citizens, often civil society organizations
    • International Observers: OAS missions, EU, Carter Center, others
    • Party Representatives: Each party can have representatives at polling centers
    • Journalists: Local and international media

    Observer Role

    • Monitor all stages of electoral process
    • Verify respect of regulations
    • Report irregularities or fraud
    • Publish reports on election quality
    • Give recommendations for improvement

    Observation Limits in Haiti

    • Insecurity prevents access to some areas
    • Observers under threat from gangs or parties
    • Lack of resources to cover whole country
    • Recommendations often not followed

    Electoral Disputes and Complaints

    Types of Complaints

    Candidates can protest for:

    • Electoral fraud (vote buying, count manipulation)
    • Violence or intimidation
    • Procedural irregularities
    • Campaign rule violations

    Complaint Procedure

    • Candidate submits complaint to CEP within legal deadline
    • CEP examines evidence
    • CEP renders decision (accept or reject)
    • If not satisfied, candidate can go to Electoral Tribunal (if exists)
    • Last resort: Superior Court of Accounts and Administrative Litigation

    Dispute Resolution Problems

    • CEP often judge and party – conflict of interest
    • Electoral Tribunal often doesn't function
    • Slow and opaque process
    • Political rather than legal decisions

    Haiti Electoral Situation 2025

    Last Elections

    • 2016: Last legislative elections held
    • 2021: Assassination of President Jovenel Moïse
    • 2024: Establishment of Transitional Presidential Council (CPT)
    • Currently: No elected president, parliament, or local officials

    Planned Elections 2025-2026

    Originally, elections were planned for:

    • November 15, 2025: First round
    • January 2026: Second round

    But in October 2025: CEP declared it impossible to organize elections before February 2026 at earliest.

    Delay Reasons

    • Insecurity: Gangs control large part of country, especially Port-au-Prince
    • Lack of Funding: No money to buy materials and pay staff
    • Electoral Infrastructure: Polling centers destroyed, no modern equipment
    • Electoral Lists: Need to update voter lists
    • Political Crisis: Disagreement among political actors on election conditions

    Current Preparations

    Despite challenges, CEP is working on:

    • Registration of 220+ regularized political parties
    • Recruiting electoral staff
    • Preparing electoral lists
    • Seeking international funding

    Necessary Reforms for Electoral Process

    1. Strengthen CEP Independence

    • Change how members chosen for more independence
    • Automatic funding so CEP doesn't depend on government
    • Legal power to sanction violations
    • Protection of CEP members against political pressure

    2. Modernize Electoral System

    • Introduce modern technology (electronic voting, rapid results transmission)
    • Digital electoral database with biometric accounts
    • Improve transparency and counting speed
    • System to verify result authenticity

    3. Strengthen Transparency

    • Publish all electoral data online
    • Allow independent observation at all stages
    • Video streaming of counting process
    • Detailed reports on each election

    4. Improve Accessibility

    • More registration centers in rural areas
    • Simplify registration process
    • Accept more forms of identification
    • Mobile services for isolated areas

    5. Combat Fraud and Violence

    • Tougher laws against vote buying and fraud
    • Rapid application of sanctions for violations
    • Protection for voters, candidates, and observers
    • Collaboration with justice to prosecute electoral criminals

    6. Electoral Education

    • Information campaigns on voter rights
    • Educate citizens on electoral process
    • Training for polling station members and observers
    • Combat disinformation

    Citizen Role in Electoral Process

    Before Elections

    • Register to vote if you haven't already
    • Inform yourself on candidates and their programs
    • Participate in debates and discussions
    • Support electoral education in your community

    During Campaign

    • Follow political news and verify information
    • Participate in rallies and political events
    • Ask candidates questions on their positions
    • Report vote buying or intimidation

    Election Day

    • Vote! It's your right and duty
    • Arrive early with your documents
    • Respect polling center rules
    • Report irregularities if you see them

    After Elections

    • Follow vote counting and results publication
    • Demand transparency if there are questions
    • Accept legal results
    • Continue monitoring elected leaders

    Conclusion

    The electoral process is the backbone of democracy. It's through free, fair, and transparent elections that citizens choose their leaders and country's direction. But in Haiti, the electoral process faces enormous challenges: insecurity, corruption, lack of resources, and lack of public trust.

    For democracy to function, Haiti needs:

    • An independent and capable CEP
    • Transparent process that people can trust
    • Security so all citizens can vote without fear
    • Adequate funding to organize quality elections
    • Political will to respect results

    But most importantly, democracy needs engaged citizens. Register. Vote. Monitor. Demand accountability. Elections are not just CEP's responsibility – they're all of ours.
