Konstitisyon Ayiti 1987

Dènye mizajou: 16 desanm 2025

Kijan Chanje Konstitisyon an?

Tit XVI defini pwosedi pou revizyon konstitisyonèl:

Etap 1: Inisyativ

Youn nan branch sa yo ka pwopoze amendman:

  • Pouvwa Egzekitif (Prezidan)
  • Pouvwa Lejislatif (Palman)
  • Inisyativ Popilè (50,000 sitwayen siyen petisyon)

Etap 2: Vote Palman

  • De chanm palman dwe vote pou chanjman an
  • Bezwen majorite 2/3 nan chak chanm

Etap 3: Piblikasyon

  • Amendman pibliye pou pèp la konnen
  • Palman an disou lè sa a (manda fini)

Etap 4: Konfime

  • Nouvo Palman eli
  • Palman sa a dwe vote ankò pou menm amendman an
  • Bezwen majorite senp

Etap 5: Referandòm (Opsyonèl)

  • Kèk atik ka mande referandòm popilè
  • Pèp la vote wi oswa non

Atik ki pa ka chanje: Atik 284 entèdi chanjman nan fòm gouvènman demokratik, sistem prezidansyèl, limit manda prezidan, ak prensip dwa moun.

Poukisa Konstitisyon Enpòtan?

1. Pwoteje Sitwayen

Konstitisyon an garanti dwa ou. Li limite sa gouvènman an ka fè kont ou.

2. Etabli Règ

Li defini kijan peyi a dwe fonksyone – ki fè sa, ki gen ki pouvwa, ki limit.

3. Prevni Abiz Pouvwa

Li divize pouvwa pou anpeche yon sèl moun oswa gwoup gen twòp pouvwa.

4. Bay Stabilite

Konstitisyon difisil pou chanje. Sa bay peyi a estabilite legal.

5. Senbolize Valè

Li reflete sa nasyon an kwè – demokrasi, libète, egalite, jistis.

Responsabilite Nou Kòm Sitwayen

Konstitisyon an sèlman bon si sitwayen yo:

  • Konnen l: Li konstitisyon an. Konprann dwa ou.
  • Defann li: Leve vwa lè gouvènman vyole l.
  • Respekte l: Suiv règ yo menm jan ou mande lòt moun fè l.
  • Patisipe: Vote, òganize, mande responsabilite.

Yon konstitisyon se pa sèlman yon dokiman – se yon angajman pèp la pran pou viv ansanm selon règ jis ak demokratik.

Konklizyon

Konstitisyon Ayiti 1987 se yon dokiman istorik ki te fèt apre deseni diktati. Li reprezante espwa pèp Ayisyen pou demokrasi, jistis, ak libète.

Malgre defi aplikasyon, konstitisyon an rete yon zouti enpòtan pou pwoteje dwa nou ak defini lòd demokratik. Travay nou se pou fè li viv – pa sèlman sou papye, men nan reyalite chak jou.

"Konstitisyon an se pou nou tout. Responsabilite pou fè l respekte se pou nou tout tou."

What is a Constitution?

A constitution is a country's supreme law – the highest rules that exist. It defines:

  • How government is organized
  • What powers each branch of government has
  • What rights citizens have
  • Limits on government power
  • How to change the constitution

All laws, decrees, and government actions must conform to the constitution. If they don't conform, they are illegal.

History of Haitian Constitutions

1804-1987: Multiple Constitutions

Since independence in 1804, Haiti has had 23 different constitutions. Each new government or regime wrote their own constitution to reflect their power.

Many of these constitutions were authoritarian – they concentrated power in the hands of one or a few people. The Duvalier dictatorship (1957-1986) used constitutions to give themselves absolute power.

1987: A New Beginning

After the fall of Jean-Claude "Baby Doc" Duvalier in 1986, the Haitian people wrote a new constitution. The 1987 Constitution symbolized:

  • Rejection of dictatorship
  • Return to democracy
  • Protection of human rights
  • Separation of powers
  • Presidential term limits

On March 29, 1987, citizens voted to approve the new constitution by referendum. This is the constitution still in effect today.

Structure of the 1987 Constitution

The Constitution has 298 articles divided into 16 titles:

  1. General Provisions – territory, environment, languages
  2. Nationality – who is Haitian
  3. Fundamental Rights – freedoms and citizen rights
  4. National Sovereignty – power of the people
  5. President of the Republic – executive power
  6. Government – prime minister, ministers
  7. National Assembly – parliament (senate and deputies)
  8. Judicial Power – courts
  9. Territorial Administration – local government
  10. Electoral Council – election organization
  11. Armed Forces – military and police
  12. Education and Teaching – education system
  13. Agriculture – land and agricultural development
  14. Work and Social Wealth – worker rights
  15. Public Finance – budget, taxes, central bank
  16. Constitutional Revision – how to change the constitution

Key Principles of the 1987 Constitution

1. Separation of Powers

The Constitution divides power among three independent branches:

Executive Power:

  • President of the Republic (elected for 5 years)
  • Prime Minister (chosen by President, approved by Parliament)
  • Ministers (cabinet)
  • Execute laws

Legislative Power:

  • Senate (30 Senators, 6-year term)
  • Chamber of Deputies (119 Deputies, 4-year term)
  • Create laws
  • Vote on budget
  • Monitor executive power

Judicial Power:

  • Supreme Court (highest court)
  • Courts of Appeal
  • Courts of First Instance
  • Peace Courts
  • Interpret laws
  • Judge cases

Each branch checks the others to prevent any single branch from having too much power.

2. Fundamental Rights and Freedoms

Title III of the Constitution is dedicated to protecting human rights. Some important articles:

Article 18 – Equality:

"All Haitians are equal before the law. The exercise of their rights is recognized without discrimination."

Article 19 – Right to Life:

"The State is obliged to guarantee the right to life for all citizens without exception."

Article 20 – Death Penalty Abolished:

"The death penalty is abolished in all cases."

Article 27 – Privacy:

"The domicile is sacred. It cannot be violated under any pretext. Searches can only be conducted in the form of law and in cases provided by law."

Article 28 – Freedom of Expression:

"All Haitians have the right to express their ideas freely in all areas by all means they choose."

Article 30 – Freedom of Religion:

"All religions and all cults are free before the law. Everyone has the right to profess their religion and practice their cult."

Article 32 – Right to Education:

"The State guarantees the right to education. It oversees the physical, intellectual, moral, professional, social, and civic education of the population."

Article 41 – Freedom of Movement:

"All Haitians have the right to establish their residence at any point in the national territory and to travel within the country."

3. Limits on Presidential Power

The 1987 Constitution was created to prevent another dictatorship. It limits presidential power:

Article 134 – Terms and Limits:

  • President serves for 5 years
  • Cannot serve two consecutive terms
  • Must wait 5 years after term ends before running again
  • Age limit: 35-70 years for candidates

Article 136 – Presidential Disarmament:

The Constitution gives Parliament power to impeach the President if they violate the law. The Senate judges the case.

4. Official Languages

Article 5:

"All Haitians have the right to speak, write, read in Creole and French. Creole and French are the official languages of the Republic."

This is a historic victory. For the first time, Creole – the language the majority of people speak – is recognized as an official language equal to French.

5. Land Ownership

Article 36:

"The right to private property is recognized. It is exercised within the limits of law. Only Haitians and Haitian companies can own property in Haiti."

Foreigners cannot buy land in Haiti. This protects national territory from foreign domination.

2011-2012 Amendments

In 2011-2012, Parliament voted to amend certain articles of the constitution. Main changes:

Dual Nationality (Article 15)

Before: Haitians who took other nationality lost their Haitian citizenship.

After: Haitians can have dual nationality without losing Haitian citizenship.

This allowed the diaspora – millions of Haitians living abroad – to keep their citizenship even if they get American, Canadian, or other passports.

Other Changes

  • Modernization of language in certain articles
  • Clarification of electoral procedures
  • Adjustments in local government organization

Constitutional Challenges in Haiti

Limited Implementation

The 1987 Constitution looks good on paper, but implementation is difficult:

  • Many articles never implemented
  • Constitutional institutions don't exist or don't function
  • Government often violates constitutional principles

Institutional Crisis

Today (2025), Haiti is in a constitutional crisis:

  • No Parliament since 2020 (terms expired without elections)
  • No elected President since 2024
  • Transitional Presidential Council (CPT) governs the country – this is not in the constitution
  • Courts are weak and not independent
  • Elections postponed indefinitely

Conflicting Interpretations

Different groups interpret the constitution differently:

  • No functioning Constitutional Court to resolve disputes
  • Ambiguous articles create debate
  • Political actors use constitution to justify their positions

Language Problem

Despite Article 5 declaring Creole an official language:

  • All laws, decrees, and judgments published only in French
  • The majority of people cannot read official documents
  • This creates a barrier to democratic participation

Important Constitutional Articles to Know

On Territory (Article 8)

"The territory of the Republic of Haiti is one and indivisible. It comprises the western part of the Island of Haiti and adjacent islands."

On Sovereignty (Article 51)

"National sovereignty resides in the universality of citizens. They cannot delegate the exercise of this sovereignty to a single individual or a single part of the population."

This means: All power comes from the people. No leader can claim to alone represent the nation.

On Elections (Article 52)

"All citizens who are eighteen (18) years of age and who enjoy civil and political rights have the right to vote. Exercise of the right to vote is obligatory."

On Political Parties (Article 31.2)

"Political parties are free to organize. They cannot be founded on religious, social, or regional basis."

On Death Penalty (Article 20)

"The death penalty is abolished in all cases."

Haiti is one of the first countries in the world to completely abolish the death penalty.

On Disarmament (Article 263)

The 1987 Constitution abolished the Haitian army (Forces Armées d'Haïti). Since 1995, Haiti has no military – only a police force.

How to Change the Constitution?

Title XVI defines the procedure for constitutional revision:

Step 1: Initiative

One of these branches can propose amendments:

  • Executive Power (President)
  • Legislative Power (Parliament)
  • Popular Initiative (50,000 citizens sign petition)

Step 2: Parliament Vote

  • Both chambers of parliament must vote for the change
  • Requires 2/3 majority in each chamber

Step 3: Publication

  • Amendment published for people to know
  • Parliament is dissolved (term ends)

Step 4: Confirmation

  • New Parliament elected
  • This Parliament must vote again for same amendment
  • Requires simple majority

Step 5: Referendum (Optional)

  • Certain articles may require popular referendum
  • People vote yes or no

Articles that cannot be changed: Article 284 prohibits changes to the democratic form of government, presidential system, presidential term limits, and human rights principles.

Why is the Constitution Important?

1. Protects Citizens

The Constitution guarantees your rights. It limits what government can do against you.

2. Establishes Rules

It defines how the country should function – who does what, who has what power, what limits.

3. Prevents Abuse of Power

It divides power to prevent one person or group from having too much power.

4. Provides Stability

Constitution is difficult to change. This gives the country legal stability.

5. Symbolizes Values

It reflects what the nation believes – democracy, freedom, equality, justice.

Our Responsibilities as Citizens

The Constitution is only good if citizens:

  • Know it: Read the constitution. Understand your rights.
  • Defend it: Speak up when government violates it.
  • Respect it: Follow the rules just as you ask others to do.
  • Participate: Vote, organize, demand accountability.

A constitution is not just a document – it's a commitment the people make to live together according to fair and democratic rules.

Conclusion

Haiti's 1987 Constitution is a historic document created after decades of dictatorship. It represents the Haitian people's hope for democracy, justice, and freedom.

Despite implementation challenges, the constitution remains an important tool to protect our rights and define democratic order. Our work is to make it live – not just on paper, but in daily reality.

"The Constitution is for all of us. The responsibility to make it respected is also for all of us."

Ki Sa Yon Konstitisyon Ye?

Yon konstitisyon se lwa sipwèm yon peyi – pi gwo règ ki egziste. Li defini:

  • Kijan gouvènman an òganize
  • Ki pouvwa chak branch gouvènman genyen
  • Ki dwa sitwayen yo genyen
  • Limit sou pouvwa gouvènman an
  • Kijan chanje konstitisyon an

Tout lwa, dekrè, ak aksyon gouvènman dwe konfòme ak konstitisyon an. Si yo pa konfòme, yo ilegal.

Istwa Konstitisyon Ayisyen

1804-1987: Plizyè Konstitisyon

Depi endepandans nan 1804, Ayiti te gen 23 konstitisyon diferan. Chak gouvènman oswa rejim nouvo te ekri konstitisyon pa yo pou reflete pouvwa yo.

Anpil konstitisyon sa yo te otoritè – yo te konsintre pouvwa nan men youn oswa kèk moun. Diktati Duvalier (1957-1986) te itilize konstitisyon pou bay tèt yo pouvwa absolit.

1987: Yon Nouvo Konmensman

Apre chit Jean-Claude "Baby Doc" Duvalier nan 1986, pèp Ayisyen an te ekri yon nouvo konstitisyon. Konstitisyon 1987 la te senbolize:

  • Rejeksyon diktati
  • Retou a demokrasi
  • Pwoteksyon dwa moun
  • Separasyon pouvwa
  • Limit tèm prezidansyèl

29 mas 1987, sitwayen yo te vote pou aprove nouvo konstitisyon an pa referandòm. Se konstitisyon sa a ki toujou an aplikasyon jounen jodi a.
