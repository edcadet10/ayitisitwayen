Gwoupman

Gwoupman are local organizations that gather people with same interests:

  • Women's groups for commerce or agriculture
  • Youth groups for sports or education
  • Peasant groups to defend their land
  • Artisan groups to sell their products

Lakou and Societies

Traditionally, lakou (space where families and neighbors live together) played important role in social organization. Secret and religious societies also served as mobilization spaces.

Steps of Community Organizing

1. Identify Problems

First step is understanding what problems the community is facing:

  • Talk to neighbors, listen to their concerns
  • Conduct surveys or community meetings
  • Observe what's happening in the neighborhood
  • Choose a specific problem to start

2. Gather People

After identifying problem, you need to mobilize people:

  • Door-to-door: Visit each house to talk with people
  • Public meetings: Call a community assembly
  • Use existing networks: Churches, schools, sports clubs
  • Social media: Create WhatsApp, Facebook, or Signal groups

3. Build Leadership

Community needs leaders from within:

  • Identify people who are respected and engaged
  • Provide training on organizing and negotiation
  • Encourage shared leadership (not just one person)
  • Ensure leaders represent community diversity

4. Develop Strategy

Together, community decides how to work:

  • What objectives do we want to achieve?
  • What resources do we have?
  • What allies can we find?
  • What actions will we take?

5. Take Action

Community mobilizes for change:

  • Direct action: Clean canals, build bridges, plant trees
  • Petitions: Collect signatures to demand government act
  • Demonstrations: Organize peaceful marches or sit-ins
  • Negotiation: Meet with authorities

6. Evaluate and Adjust

After action, community looks at what worked and what didn't:

  • Did we achieve our objective?
  • What lessons did we learn?
  • What can we improve?
  • What are next steps?

Community Organizing Strategies

Door-to-Door Mobilization

Going house by house to talk with people directly:

  • Create personal relationships with people in community
  • Listen to individual concerns
  • Invite people to meetings
  • Identify potential leaders

Community Assemblies

Organize meetings where everyone can participate:

  • Choose accessible location for everyone
  • Announce meeting in advance
  • Create space where everyone feels they can speak
  • Make decisions by consensus or vote

Information Campaigns

Educate community on important issues:

  • Distribute flyers or posters
  • Use community radio
  • Create social media content
  • Organize presentations and discussions

Direct Action

Take concrete action to solve problems:

  • Community does work they can do themselves
  • Show community capacity and commitment
  • Create visible and rapid results
  • Build confidence and momentum

Advocacy and Negotiation

Work with authorities to get change:

  • Prepare solid case with data and clear demands
  • Create public pressure if necessary
  • Negotiate for solutions that benefit everyone
  • Follow up to ensure commitments are respected

Examples of Community Organizing in Haiti

OPODNE: Northeast Organization

OPODNE works with 56 communities in Northeast department:

  • Defend peasant rights to their land
  • Promote sustainable agriculture and agroecology
  • Resist projects that displace peasants
  • Strengthen local organizational capacity

MPNKP: National Peasant Movement

MPNKP works in 10 departments on multiple projects:

  • Pig repopulation: Give peasants pigs to develop livestock
  • Agroecology: Training on agriculture without chemicals
  • Local seeds: Protect and distribute traditional seeds
  • Land defense: Fight against peasant land grabbing

Tèt Kole Ti Peyizan: Food Sovereignty

Tèt Kole network mobilizes peasants to defend their rights:

  • Fight for food sovereignty (peasants' right to choose what they plant)
  • Resistance against GMOs and monopolized seeds
  • Defense of local markets against imported products
  • Promote consumption of local products

PetroChallenge Movement 2018

PetroChallenge movement is one of the largest mobilizations against corruption:

  • Millions in streets demanding account of $4 billion PetroCaribe
  • Organized by youth groups, activists, and civil society
  • Used hashtag #KotKòbPetwoKaribea to mobilize
  • Despite persecution, movement continues demanding justice

Neighborhood Committees for Security

In some neighborhoods, people organize to protect themselves:

  • Create community watch systems
  • Clean streets to eliminate criminal hiding spots
  • Collaborate with local police (where possible)
  • Support violence victims

Community Organizing Challenges in Haiti

Insecurity and Gangs

Gang violence limits people's capacity to organize:

  • Community leaders under threat or forced to leave
  • Difficult to gather people safely
  • In gang-controlled areas, organization impossible
  • Fear prevents people from engaging

Poverty and Survival

When people are struggling to survive, difficult to engage:

  • People don't have time because they're busy finding livelihood
  • No resources to support organization
  • Immediate needs more important than long-term objectives

Political Repression

Authorities often don't like community organizing:

  • Intimidate leaders who criticize government
  • Abusive arrests to discourage mobilization
  • Use force to crush demonstrations
  • Buy leaders to divide movement

Internal Divisions

Communities not always united:

  • Political differences create tension
  • Competition for limited resources
  • Lack of trust between different groups
  • Leaders seeking personal power rather than common good

Lack of Structural Support

Community organizations lack institutional support:

  • No laws protecting organizational rights
  • Government doesn't support local initiatives
  • Difficult to find funding
  • Lack of public spaces for meetings

How to Start Organizing in Your Community

Step 1: Listen and Learn

  • Talk to neighbors to understand their concerns
  • Identify problems affecting many people
  • Search to know what resources already exist
  • Learn history of organizing in the area

Step 2: Start Small

  • Don't try to solve all problems together
  • Choose a specific problem you can achieve
  • Start with a small group of engaged people
  • Create a quick victory to build momentum

Step 3: Build Relationships

  • Spend time with people, not just in meetings
  • Create trust through consistent actions
  • Respect everyone's opinions
  • Look for common ground, not differences

Step 4: Develop Capacity

  • Learn about organizing, negotiation, advocacy
  • Share skills among group members
  • Invite experts for training
  • Learn from other successful movements

Step 5: Take Action and Persist

  • Move from talking to doing
  • Don't be discouraged if first action doesn't succeed
  • Learn from failures and adjust
  • Celebrate victories, even small ones

Tools for Community Organizing

Social Media

  • WhatsApp/Signal: Discussion groups and rapid mobilization
  • Facebook: Community page and events
  • Twitter/X: Share information and create attention
  • YouTube: Educational videos and documentaries

Community Radio

  • Reach people not on internet
  • Discuss local problems in language people speak
  • Create platform for community voice

Meetings and Forums

  • Space for democratic discussion
  • Create consensus on objectives
  • Strengthen bonds between members

Petitions and Surveys

  • Collect signatures to show support
  • Document problems and demands
  • Create pressure on authorities

Demonstrations and Marches

  • Public visibility for demands
  • Show strength and unity
  • Create political pressure

Protect Your Organization

Physical Security

  • Always meet in public and safe places
  • Alert family where you're going
  • If threatened, change meeting location
  • Work in groups, not alone

Digital Security

  • Use secure apps like Signal
  • Don't share sensitive information on regular WhatsApp
  • Protect phone with strong password
  • Be careful of spies in your group

Legal Security

  • Know your rights to protest and organize
  • Keep records of all actions
  • Have contact with lawyer or human rights organization
  • Report intimidation to national organizations

Protection Against Division

  • Establish clear principles and objectives from start
  • Create processes to resolve conflicts
  • Transparency in money management and decisions
  • Don't let one person make all decisions

Connection Between Local and National Organizations

Networks and Federations

Local organizations stronger when connected:

  • Share information and strategies
  • Support other movements in other regions
  • Create national pressure for local demands
  • Learn from others' successes and failures

Allies with National Organizations

  • Human rights NGOs can provide legal support
  • Unions can provide organizing expertise
  • Churches and religious groups can offer space and protection
  • Media can provide visibility

Conclusion

Community organizing is the most powerful tool the Haitian people have to change their situation. Our history is full of examples of successful mobilization – from the 1804 revolution to the PetroChallenge movement.

Change doesn't come from above. It comes from below, when ordinary people gather to defend their interests. Konbit, gwoupman, and our solidarity traditions show we already know how to organize.

Your community has power. But this power only exists when we unite, organize, and act together. Start where you are. Gather your neighbors. Identify problems. Take action. Change starts with you.
