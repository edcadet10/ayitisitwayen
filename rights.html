Article 18: Equality Before the Law

"All Haitians are equal before the law. The exercise of their rights is recognized without discrimination."

This means:

  • Everyone has the same legal rights
  • Law must treat everyone the same
  • Discrimination is illegal
  • No one is above the law

Article 19: Right to Life

"The State is obliged to guarantee the right to life for all citizens without exception."

The right to life is the most fundamental right. Without life, you cannot enjoy other rights. The State has a responsibility to:

  • Protect citizens' lives
  • Provide security
  • Prevent murder and assassination
  • Punish those who violate the right to life

Article 20: Death Penalty Abolished

"The death penalty is abolished in all cases."

Haiti is one of the first countries in the world to completely abolish the death penalty. This confirms respect for the right to life – even people who commit serious crimes deserve to live.

Categories of Rights

1. Civil Rights

Civil rights protect individual freedom and personal security.

Article 24: Personal Freedom

"Individual freedom is guaranteed and protected by the State."
  • No one can arrest you without evidence
  • If they arrest you, you have the right to know why
  • You have the right to get a lawyer
  • You cannot stay in prison without trial

Article 27: Privacy

"The domicile is sacred. It cannot be violated under any pretext."
  • Police cannot search your home without court warrant
  • Your privacy is protected
  • Your correspondence (letters, phone, email) cannot be illegally intercepted

Article 28: Freedom of Expression

"All Haitians have the right to express their ideas freely in all areas by all means they choose."
  • You can speak your mind
  • You can write, publish, disseminate your ideas
  • Press is free – no censorship
  • You can criticize government without fear

Article 28.1: Freedom of Assembly

"Freedom to meet and freedom to associate are recognized."
  • You can organize peaceful demonstrations
  • You can create or join organizations
  • You can gather to express your opinion

Article 30: Freedom of Religion

"All religions and all cults are free before the law."
  • You can choose your religion or have none
  • No one can force you into a religion
  • You can practice your belief in peace
  • State respects all religions equally

Article 41: Freedom of Movement

"All Haitians have the right to establish their residence at any point in the national territory and to travel within the country."
  • You can travel freely in the country
  • You can live where you want
  • You have the right to leave the country and return

2. Political Rights

Political rights allow you to participate in governing the country.

Article 52: Right to Vote

"All citizens who are eighteen (18) years of age and who enjoy civil and political rights have the right to vote."
  • Vote to choose your leaders
  • Run for elective office
  • Participate in referendums

Article 31.2: Political Freedom

  • Right to believe in any political ideology
  • Right to form or join political parties
  • Right to campaign for your candidate
  • Right to peaceful opposition

3. Economic Rights

Economic rights concern work, property, and economic activity.

Article 35: Right to Work

"The State recognizes the right of all citizens to work."
  • Right to work without discrimination
  • Right to fair wages
  • Right to safe working conditions
  • Right to form unions

Article 35.1: Minimum Wage

"The State establishes minimum wage according to cost of living and working conditions."
  • Workers have right to a wage that allows them to live
  • Employers cannot pay less than legal minimum

Article 36: Right to Property

"The right to private property is recognized. It is exercised within the limits of law."
  • You have the right to own property
  • No one can take your property without compensation
  • Only Haitians can own land in Haiti

4. Social Rights

Social rights concern education, health, and social welfare.

Article 32: Right to Education

"The State guarantees the right to education. It oversees the physical, intellectual, moral, professional, social, and civic education of the population."
  • All children have the right to go to school
  • Basic education must be free and mandatory
  • State must create public schools

Article 32.1: Free School

"The first degree of schooling is mandatory under penalty of sanctions."

Article 19: Right to Health

"The State has an obligation to guarantee the right to health for all citizens."
  • Access to basic healthcare
  • Public hospitals and clinics
  • Protection against epidemics

Article 22: Right to Social Security

  • Assistance for those in need
  • Protection for children, elderly, and disabled
  • Social insurance for workers

5. Cultural Rights

Cultural rights protect heritage and cultural identity.

Article 5: Official Languages

"Creole and French are the official languages of the Republic."
  • Right to speak your language
  • Right to receive education in Creole
  • Right to get official information in Creole

Article 215: Heritage Protection

  • Right to participate in cultural life
  • Right to practice your traditions
  • Protection of historic and artistic sites

Challenges and Rights Violations in Haiti

Insecurity and Gang Violence

Today, armed gangs control large parts of Haiti's territory, especially in Port-au-Prince. This creates serious rights violations:

  • Right to life violated: Over 5,000 people killed each year by gang violence
  • Freedom of movement limited: People cannot travel freely due to blockades and insecurity
  • Right to property violated: Gangs force people to leave their homes, loot their property
  • Right to education violated: Over 900 schools closed due to insecurity

Violence Against Women

Rape and sexual violence have become weapons of war for gangs:

  • Thousands of rape cases each year
  • Women cannot go to work or school due to fear
  • Justice not available for victims
  • Generalized impunity

Weak Justice System

Legal rights are not respected because the justice system doesn't function:

  • Illegal detention: 75% of prisoners have no trial – they stay years in prison without conviction
  • Limited access to justice: Poor people cannot afford lawyers
  • Corrupt courts: Judges and magistrates often corrupt
  • Impunity: Criminals not punished, victims don't get justice

Economic Rights Neglected

  • Education: 50% of children don't go to school
  • Health: Hospitals don't function, medications not available
  • Work: Unemployment around 60%, minimum wage not enforced
  • Housing: Millions live in precarious houses, without access to drinking water

Discrimination

Despite Article 18 guaranteeing equality, discrimination exists:

  • Class discrimination: Rich elite enjoy rights, poor people suffer
  • Sex discrimination: Women paid less, have fewer opportunities
  • Language discrimination: People who speak only Creole excluded from politics and justice
  • Discrimination against street children: Restavèk, homeless children violated every day

Rights Protection Mechanisms

National Institutions

Office of Citizen Protection (OPC):

  • Created to receive complaints about rights violations
  • Can conduct investigations and recommendations
  • But lacks funding and real power

Courts:

  • Have responsibility to protect rights
  • But corrupt and influenced by politics

National Police (PNH):

  • Should protect citizens
  • But often accused of rights violations too

Civil Society

Local and international organizations work to defend rights:

  • RNDDH (National Network for Defense of Human Rights in Haiti)
  • FONHDILAC (Haitian Foundation for Defense of Freedom)
  • IJDH (Institute for Justice & Democracy in Haiti)
  • BAI (Bureau des Avocats Internationaux)
  • Many other community and religious groups

International

  • UN Human Rights: United Nations Mission in Haiti
  • OAS: Inter-American Commission on Human Rights
  • Amnesty International
  • Human Rights Watch

What Can You Do to Defend Your Rights?

1. Know Your Rights

  • Read the Constitution, especially Title III
  • Learn what rights you have
  • Understand what limits government has

2. Document Violations

  • If your rights are violated, write all details
  • Take photos if possible
  • Get witnesses
  • Keep evidence

3. Report

  • File case at Office of Citizen Protection
  • Contact human rights organizations
  • Inform media if appropriate

4. Organize

  • Join community groups that defend rights
  • Create collectives to defend rights in your neighborhood
  • Use collective strength to demand justice

5. Demand Change

  • Vote for candidates who respect human rights
  • Participate in peaceful demonstrations
  • Hold leaders accountable
  • Support justice and security reform

Children's Rights in Haiti

Children have special rights that protect them:

Article 260: Child Protection

  • Right to name, nationality, free education
  • Protection against exploitation
  • Protection against mistreatment
  • Access to healthcare

Real Problems:

  • 300,000 children in restavèk system (domestic slavery)
  • Thousands of children have no birth certificate
  • Sexual abuse against children at high levels
  • Children recruited by armed gangs

Women's Rights in Haiti

Article 17: Sex Equality

"Women and men have the same rights and the same obligations in all areas."

Legal Rights:

  • Right to vote (since 1950)
  • Right to work without discrimination
  • Right to property
  • Right to run for any position

Real Challenges:

  • Systematic rape as weapon of war
  • Domestic violence at high levels
  • Low political representation (less than 5% of parliament)
  • Wage gap between women and men

Conclusion

Rights in Haiti are guaranteed on paper in the 1987 Constitution, but their implementation is limited. Insecurity, corruption, and a weak justice system create a situation where rights are violated every day.

But hope exists. Active civil society, courageous journalists, and ordinary citizens continue to fight for their rights. Each time someone raises their voice against injustice, each time a community organizes to defend their rights, each time we demand accountability – we are closer to a Haiti where everyone's rights are respected.

"Rights are not a gift – they are a struggle. We must fight every day to keep them and advance them."
